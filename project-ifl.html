Designing an interactive forest of lights for a winter playground

The scope

This project was the final deliverable of the course Physical Interaction Design. During 5 intense weeks (from September to mid-October) we worked to realise a small scale model that followed the theme of designing for a winter playground:


"The task is to develop a piece of interactive playground equipment for the Swedish winter situation. You will have to explore the design space of bringing interactive technology outdoors, focusing on technical as well as experiential challenges of designing large scale interactive systems for cold, dark and wet play settings."

Assessing the playground situation

The project was kickstarted through a bodystorming session, where we were assigned to carry out a visit to a playground at nighttime. The purpose of this session was to reconnect with playgrounds and gain an understanding of the place in conditions similar to the Swedish winter, when daylight in non-existent past 3 p.m.

The challenge: Bring life to playgrounds in winter

An issue that rose during the playground visit was the extremely poor lighting, making the place seem both unfriendly and unsafe - far from the adventurous environment it is in daylight. From this background, and along with research into interactive play, our team defined our goals:


  1. Bring more light to playgrounds and find new innovative ways of making the lighting of playgrounds a part of the play experience.
  2. Allow for an open-ended play setting, while still adding a sense of guided play and mystery to engage users in the interaction.
  3. Promote social interaction and collaboration among playground visitors.

The team / my role

Ideation, prototyping, construction of the model, user experience and interaction design were all done in collaboration with my teammates Okra-Livia Inkeri Dimle, Melissa Obrou, and Vincent Giardina.


Apart from the above, my responsibilities also involved the setup of the technology (constructing the circuit, connecting sensors and LED-strip), as well as the programming of the light scripts and the interaction with the sensors.

The Design

Our final design was inspired by the white winter landscape and how it reflects and disperses light. As for the shape of the trees in our model, we aimed at more fairytale-like style, inspired by the mushroom forest of Alice in Wonderland. This shape was also favoured for its potential to work as a shelter from harsh weather conditions and heavy rain, as well as a possibility of creating a protective shell over the lights and electronics. The lights were inspired by the movie Avatar and the nighttime colours of the forest of Pandora.

The model

The model was built on a 900 SEK budget, where most of the costs went into the strip of 200 addressable NeoPixel LEDs. Together with borrowed and scrap materials, the trees were constructed using chicken wire, papier mache and white paint. The electronics included a LED-strip soldered the desired length, an Arduino board, flex sensors, and a passive infrared motion sensor.

The technology

The interactive part of the installation was made through the creation of a light script for the Arduino board. The code was written using C/C++ and listens to the installation's 4 sensors (three flex sensors, one infrared motion sensor). Depending on which sensor is interacted with, the code fires its corresponding light script. To give the user a hint of where the sensors are located, all sensors had their own personal light signal.


We also implemented a 5 second timer, which allowed the triggering additional light scripts when two or more sensors were interacted with at approximately the same time. This is how we implemented the “communication” among the trees; light scripts that ran only when multiple sensors were triggered.

Fulfilling the goals

By allowing the illumination of playgrounds to become a part of the play experience, we can create a more engaging experience for its visitors, prolonging it use, especially in wintertime when daylight is scarce.


Turning the light installation into a game, where visitors can explore different ways to get the forest to light up, allowed our solution to span a larger target group - both young and older children (or even parents) can engage in the interaction, at their own preferred level. Finally, the use of the many sensors pushes visitors to interact and collaborate with each other in order to get the forest to light up fully.

Learning outcomes

Throughout the design process, we had the possibility to test our installation with children of different ages. This led to the addition of the pearl curtain, to encourage more use of movement when interacting with the installation. We also found the children were particularly mesmerized by the prism-like effect when the pearls refract the light.


At the final exhibition of the Physical Interaction Design course, people of all ages engaged in our installation. We found that the visitors spent a lot of time at the installation, trying to trigger the many light scripts. People particularly enjoyed the "full forest dance" with multi-coloured lights, but had somewhat of a hard time figuring out what triggered it, however this lead to a more playful enivronment among the users, trying to solve the problem together.
