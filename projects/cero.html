Rethinking the
CERO Challenge.

Critical Design, User Experience

Since the introduction of persuasive design into the development of modern technologies, researchers have considered how aspects of persuasion may be incorporated to push the user of the technology to do good. One of the areas that has seen the application of such attempts is apps developed to tackle behaviours related to the ongoing climate crisis.

This project explored the use of persuasive strategies from a critical design perspective. Through the evaluation and redesign of the CERO Challenge app, developed to promote pro-environmental travel behaviours, we considered which strategies align with long term pro-environmental behaviour change and how they affect the design.

Project Setting

This project was a part of the Advanced Project Course in Interactive Media Technology, where the organisation CERO (Climate and Economic Research in Organizations) provided the project brief.

Team & Personal Contribution

Working alongside classmates Diego Martin, Fredilyn Villarin, Marco Koivisto and Rafael Lucena, the project was conducted under the guidance of our supervisor Elina Eriksson, who brought expertise within ICT for sustainability.

While all five of us contributed in all aspects of the project, Diego and Marco took the design lead. Being highly engaged in behavioural psychology and its relation to modern app and web development, I took the scientific lead and conducted most of the research that the project was based on top of.

About the Project

The mission of CERO is to help companies find economically sustainable ways of reducing their emissions, particularly with focus on the travel habits of the company’s workforce. CERO Challenge is an app developed by the team behind CERO, which had the purpose of tracking individual travel behaviour and to enable users of the app to challenge each other to reduce emissions. At its current state it does not have a connection to the CERO organisation and is no longer in use.

Our team was given the challenge to help the client resurrect the app, turning it to a bottom-up tool to help companies raise awareness and engagement in their CERO goals on employee level. And in doing so, exploring alternative motivations, besides the common gamification and engagement tactics, that could drive the application.

Screenshots of the application prior to the redesign.

Challenges

The previous application struggled with intuitive navigation and the user was repetitively asked to log the data of each trip. A first objective was therefore to make this process less cumbersome for the user, while still keeping the data accurate.

The app also struggled with displaying its data to the user in a meaningful way. We therefore sought to explore multiple ways in which travel data could be presented to not only making travel impact easier to understand, but to do so in a way that prompted the user to engage in reducing their travel impact. We interviewed several stakeholders that were involved in the implementation of CERO at their workplace, to gain an understanding of their ideal expectations on the app. Both as someone who wanted to spur their colleagues engagement, but also as with themselves as the user.

Being out of use, it also needed an aesthetic update to adhere to app design guidelines. The client also wished to more tightly connect the app with the organisations work, including a leaderboard of the top performing companies. But foremost, we needed to tackle the challenge of making the app promote and encourage pro-environmental behaviour change, and that was not an easy feat!

How do we promote
pro-environmental behaviours?

Making use of technology to promote pro-environmental behaviours is a difficult task for several reasons. Today, many modern apps make use of persuasive design to catch and keep the user’s attention. This includes features such as attention-grabbing colours, notifications, heavily personalised content and gamification. However, researchers exploring the field of persuasive design used for promoting sustainability [1, 2] have found that many of these features significantly clash with attempts to encourage pro-environmental behaviours.

As a part of our project we dealt with the challenges introduced by Schwartz’s circumplex structure of values [3], particularly self-enhancement vs. self-transcendence values and its implications for understanding human motivation.

Apps developed in Western societies, just like the these societies in general, are highly permeated by self-enhancement values, i.e. values related to the welfare of oneself. Such as pursuing one’s own interests and concern for one’s own success, where social comparison and recognition are highly valued. Self-enhancement values are also known to undermine pro-environmental behaviour change. On the other hand, self-transcendence values; the welfare and interest in others, are known for bringing about pro-environmental behaviours through for example social facilitation and cooperation. And it was with this as our perspective that we approached the design.

A Figma canvas with early stage mockups.

Solutions

To reduce some of the repetitiveness of the app (the user having to personally log each trip) we decided to aim for easiness over perfect accuracy. Rather than aiming for automatic tracking solutions, we worked from the basis of us humans as highly habitual; most of us have very distinct travel patterns to and from work. Asking the user for their travel habits once (with the option to update this in the app settings later) makes the experience more convenient.

It was clear from the link to the clients work with the organisation that we would be working with carbon calculation of some sort. At the current standpoint in time sustainability is often presented to users as either side of a coin: this is the kilograms of CO2 you have wasted, or, this is the kilograms of CO2 you saved. But what difference does a kg of CO2 make? And what implications do the words “waste” versus “save” have on the user making the effort? We examined these questions with our interviewees and found positive reinforcement to be the way forward. Relating to nature’s natural carbon savers ‒ trees, was the preferred way of materialising carbon emission savings into something comprehensible.

We shifted the core of the app to be all about community. Joining challenges together and endorsing one another to bring in self-transcendence values; social facilitation and cooperation.

App setup for increased convenience.

Prompting the user for the nitty-gritty details only once makes for a more pleasant experience.

Team challenges to promote cooperation.

Rather than the one-to-one competition style, we aimed for cooperation through enabling employees to join in on the company defined challenge of their choice.

While the old version made other people your competitors, the new one focuses on people working together towards a goal, contributing to each other’s successes.

Feed built for social facilitation.

To add a sense of community and enabling users to endorse each other, a feed was created for users’ achievements. Posts are autogenerated when a user contributes to a challenge, but can be edited and personalised, and it’s up to the user whether to post them or not.

Emphasis on the collective contribution.

"Alone we can do so little; together we can do so much”‒while we let some gamification aspects persist, we shifted the focus onto the collective contribution through displaying team effort first.

Leaderboards tend to encourage the ones at the very top, and discourage the ones at the bottom. By highlighting the total contribution we shift the focus toward communal effort.

Lessons learned

For me, this project was particularly enthralling to take on because it gave us the opportunity to think about our design choices from a new perspective. It is so easy to go along with established design and persuasion tactics employed in most applications today, without critically evaluating what motivations they build on. And in extension to that, how they can shape our way of thinking and behaving.

This project was also extremely challenging because we had to find design solutions aiming to promote values which our Western society, to most part, does not regularly reflect over or attend to.

Outcomes

Our final design was very positively received by the client as well as the sustainable ICT research community at KTH. It was praised for its new approach to tackling pro-environmental behaviour change and for its attempt to balance out the “negative” drivers and motivators commonly employed in apps with the focus on communal efforts and social support.

Two members of our team continued working with the client in their master thesis projects, to further examine the potential of the app with users.
