Master Thesis:

Visualizing 5G data

or, "Information visualization of network parameters in private cellular network solutions", but that's quite a mouthful!

About

In the final project towards my M.Sc.Eng degree I set out to explore information visualization in the context of 5G. Together with telecom company Telia, I researched what the future of private cellular networks may look like for industrial enterprises.

Particularly, this project set out to explore the affordances and challenges with information visualization of data in 5G networks as a mean to communicate and showcase 5G’s private network solutions to industry enterprises.

The Future with 5G

There has been a lot of buzz surrounding 5G ‒ the next generation of cellular networks, which with its great increase in data speeds promises the arrival of our new, highly connected future.

With 5G comes the promise of cellular networks that can cater to a much larger number of connected devices, deal with a vast increase in data traffic and deliver response times remarkably close to real-time. 5G is therefore very likely to play a key role in what has been coined the 4th industrial revolution: the increased connection of IoT devices to manufacturing processes to increase both automation and data-driven decision making in industry.

Yet, that brief summary probably did very little to help you paint a picture of what 5G may look like in practice!

A tiny snippet of the colour coded madness that made up my research document. The antidote of a sight for sore eyes, but a structured delight for this researcher. 😎

Giving Private Cellular Networks a Face

Latency, throughput, availability… there are lots of technical terms associated with cellular networks. While these may be very familiar to the trained network operator and the hobby enthusiast on Internet connectivity, they may not say much to the average joe.

With the introduction of 5G comes the increased potential of private cellular networks ‒ network solutions separated from the general public (and with it, the issues that come with shared traffic usage). The private cellular solution allows for another level of tailoring and optimisation of the network to the needs of the enterprise.

These network solutions are typically promoted by speaking of numbers. “Latency will be down to 1 millisecond compared to the 20-200 milliseconds we see with 4G today”, “throughput will increase by 100 times”, “5G will have 99.999% availability”. But speaking solely of numbers rarely paints the picture!

Information visualization is one tool we can use to give these technical terms a new life and added meaning to the enterprise customer looking to invest in such a solution.

“Private cellular networks give organizations the freedom to make technical and economic choices that best suit their requirements.”
‒ Ken Davidson, Cisco blog

The Challenge

Dashboards are today the common tool for utilising information visualization in enterprises, often referred to as Business Intelligence tools. They are not necessarily new for the manufacturing industries either, or within network management. The interest and challenge here lie between the two: when network management becomes a key player in industry performance.

Likely, the importance of understanding and managing the network will move beyond the traditional network operator. This means we need to find new ways of communicating the data, making it easy to grasp even for the “network newbie”. And in doing so, we can also provide a visual selling point for investing in a complex technological solution!

In this project, I sought to define the affordances and challenges of such use of information visualization in a dashboard tool for showcasing 5G’s private cellular networks.

Process

From a product design point of view the process of this project might look a little curious, as perhaps should be! The process of this project also highly reflects its associated scientific report, that is the traditional Introduction ‒ Theory and Related Work ‒ Research Question ‒ Method ‒ Result ‒ Discussion ‒ Conclusion steps of academic research.

  1. Research. Research. Research. This project was just as much about the academic paper as the project itself. To build a solid foundation to work from, I investigated 5G technology and its expected impact on industry.

  2. Selection of key network parameters. Based on the knowledge acquired about 5G, I picked five concepts that clearly captured the benefits that 5G brings and are likely to come of key importance to industries moving forward.

  3. Data gathering. In order to visualize the five selected parameters ‒ throughput, latency, availability, coverage and connected devices ‒ I needed to understand how they are measured and reported. What it looks like today and how it might expand with 5G tomorrow.

  4. Best practices and SotA. The second part to visualizing something, apart from having data to feed the visualization with, is of course understanding how to best do it! This meant diving into material such as Stephen Few’s “Information Dashboard Design” and Tableau Software’s whitepapers on visualization. I also reviewed a large number of dashboards used within manufacturing industries, in network management and IoT to get the full picture of the current state of development.

  5. Ideation and defining hypotheses. Based on the data gathered and the best practices reviewed, I did a brainstorm session to define hypotheses on how each network parameter is best visualized to convey its meaning and value in relation to 5G. This step was continuously reiterated in conversation with my supervisors.

  6. Development and expert review of the research prototype. Here’s where you might have expected a user evaluation and didn’t find one! Working with such an into-the-future-product, it is hard to work based on an intended end-user when so much of the field remains unknown. Instead, I began evaluating the prototype with experts within UX with previous experience of developing tools in similar areas. This with the goal of maximising how communicative the visualizations were in relation to 5G, to ease engagement with industry.

  7. Industry interview. Having gathered tons of helpful feedback from the expert review I set out to discuss dashboard tools and visualization with industry to further discover and refine affordances and challenges. The interview focused on the current application of dashboard tools in industry, how the network plays part in how manufacturing processes run today, and what role the developed visualizations may play moving forward.

  8. Suggested design. Based on the expert review and notes from the industry interview, a final mock-up was made to aid the findings with a more visual example. The idea of the mock-up was also to generate a sense of direction for the future of the project, and a clearer product idea that could be used in conversation with enterprises to continue a more user-driven development of the tool.

A digital reiteration on the earlier coverage sketches. A combination of coverage heatmap and circles for representing the radius of each network station (orange dots).

Research Prototype

While information visualizations in part can be showcased through various low to high fidelity prototypes, their concepts are hard to communicate through limited interactions. With the projects main purpose to properly evaluate information visualization as a tool, the prototype was directly built in a more Minimum Viable Product form.

The prototype was developed using the JavaScript component library React, alongside the visualization library D3. The Research Prototype was centred around finding the optimal way to represent the five selected network parameters and can be viewed in the demo presentation below.

Findings

Below I have listed the four most major findings, which consequently also had the most influence on the second iteration:

  1. Developed visualizations were successful in terms of communicating the meaning of each parameter (i.e. what is throughput, what is latency etc). However, for the “network newbie”, this might not be enough to understand what value 5G’s private cellular networks bring.

  2. Visualizing the network parameters in a relatable context (such as the use of maps and calendars) may be a way to make these parameters more accessible for the “network newbies”.

  3. A clear preference for the ‘Coverage and Devices’ view. This suggests that putting the parameters in a spatial context ‒ seeing not only how they vary over time, but also over geographical space ‒ could be a promising way forward in terms of making the tool valuable for industry.

  4. Get more action-centred. Successful tools today are very action centric-and it is unclear how the visualizations might fit in in such a context. The views are currently great for analytical work over time, but it remains unclear whether a user would visit these parameters aside from when an error occurs. The context of what went wrong, where did it go wrong and what action must I, the user, take to resolve the issue? would likely be a highly valuable addition.

Summarising the findings, it is clear that the proposed tool needs to get better at communicating the industry fit, i.e. how these parameters relate and integrate into industry operations. Visual representations of this type of early demonstration of ideas are likely to be highly valuable in terms of engaging and facilitating conversations with industry and increasing the likelihood of future successes in the development of a dashboard tool for 5G’s private cellular network solutions.

A Brief Reiteration

To sum up the findings more visually, and to provide some guidance of what direction future development of a dashboard tool for 5G’s private cellular networks could take, a brief reiteration was made.

As I hope you, the reader, likely has understood by now, the state of still attempting to predict the future with 5G shaped this project to be more of an exploration than a final design! Therefore, below follows a more "refined" prototype to provide ideas for how a dashboard tool may serve private 5G networks in the future. As well as a take on how the tool explored in this thesis could become more action-centric:

Connecting the views.

Combining the visualizations into a singular view and connecting them to the site map will make the network easy to overview and monitor.

This may also aid the communication of the value with 5G network solutions by providing such a clear illustration of the big game changer with 5G: monitoring devices in real-time.

Linking it all together.

Connecting the visualisation of network parameters to the performance of a singular device (here, a base station) might aid both network troubleshooting and the understanding of the different parameters and their impact on performance.

Action-centric with alerts.

Equipping the tool with alerts for quick identification of issues in the network, alongside visualisations (like the node-to-node latency) can aid troubleshooting and is likely to be a key functionality for making the tool more action-centric and valuable.

Heatmaps were also considered for identifying bottlenecks, but it remains unknown to what extent there is sufficient data to fulfill such functionality.
