Project Setting

This project was the final deliverable in the course Physical Interaction Design and included a fully developed concept alongside a small-scale physical interactive model for an exhibition at KTH.

Team & Personal Contribution

Ideation, prototyping, construction of the model, user experience and interaction design were all done in collaboration with my teammates Melissa Obrou, Okra-Livia Inkeri Dimle and Vincent Giardina.

Apart from the above, my contribution also involved the setup of the technology (i.e. constructing the circuit, connecting the sensors and LED-strip) as well as the programming of the script and the interaction with the sensors.

About the Project

The theme for the project challenge was “Designing for a winter playground”. Our team was tasked with designing a piece of interactive playground equipment fit for outdoor play in the Swedish winter.

Playing outside no matter the season or the weather is a part of the Swedish mentality (as the saying goes “there is no bad weather, only bad clothes”). However, with the winter months lack of daylight past 3 p.m. and playgrounds remaining poorly lit throughout the year, there is a lot that can be done to improve the outdoor play environment.

The purpose of this project was to explore how playgrounds could be fitted with interactive technology, as well as the challenges of designing large scale systems for cold, dark and wet play settings.

Some sketches that were made prior to the development of the model.

Challenges

Poor lighting at playgrounds make them seem both unfriendly and unsafe, far from the adventurous environment they provide in daylight. Therefore we needed to figure out a way of bringing more lights into the environment. Rather than just considering the lights an inanimate object we wanted them to be integrated into the playfulness of the place.

Playgrounds are highly active environments, meant for running around and having fun. Whatever solution we would land in would have to encourage this sense of movement, but we also knew that the winters often bring sudden downpour of rain or snow or a even the mix of both. Another challenge was therefore to consider how our solution could function under those conditions and in what ways we could include shelter.

Finally, playgrounds are multifunctional environments. They not only need to be able to cater to both individual and social play settings, but to the needs of kids at varying ages. A 1-year-old and a 7-year-old are not likely to approach a playground with the same eyes. Therefore, we wanted our design to allow for an open-ended play setting, while still adding a sense of guided play and mystery to also engage older children in the interaction.

Different stages of the model development.

The Model

The small-scale model was built on a 900 SEK budget, where most of costs went into the 200 addressable LED-lights that were used. Together with borrowed and scrap materials, the trees were constructed using chicken wire, papier mache and white paint. The electronics included an Arduino board, flex sensors and a passive infrared motion sensor.

Tech

The interactive part of the installation was made through the creation of a simple program for the Arduino board. The code was written using C/C++ and listened to the installation’s 4 sensors (3 flex sensors, 1 infrared motion sensor). Depending on which and how many of the sensors the user interacted with, the code fired corresponding light scripts.

Inspiration

When it came to inspiration for our final design, we focused a lot on the small joys of childhood, such as waking up to find a white winter landscape outside your window. How the snow reflects and disperses light, making the whole world sparkle.

As for the shape of the trees in our model, we aimed at a more fairytale-like style, taking inspiration from the mushroom forest of Alice in Wonderland. A shape that was also favoured for its potential to also act as shelter from harsh weather conditions.

Our way of making use of the lights was inspired by the movie Avatar and the nighttime colours of the forest of Pandora, as well as modern day art installations such as the Supertree Grove at the Gardens by the Bay in Singapore.

A presentation of the concept.

Solutions

Our final concept landed in the idea of an artificial winter forest that lights up when interacted with. Every single tree is meant to be unique and different types of interaction will trigger them. Some you might simply need to approach (motion sensors) while others may react to touch, such as finding and pressing a button or hugging the tree (pressure sensors).

The interaction with the forest can be seen as a game, where you have to figure out how to activate each tree, and perhaps discover through play that when more than one tree is activated at the same time ‒ new exciting things happen. This was also a way for us to attempt to meet both individual and social play needs as well as engaging children at different stages of cognitive development. The youngest children may just engage in pressing a button and be mesmerized by the light show. Whereas older children can grasp and play the game; running around and trying to get the whole forest to light up at once.

Through connecting the trees and having them "communicate", we think that the installation could spur both movement and social interaction. The idea for the installation is that it is meant to be very tactile, as this is an important aspect of how children interact with the world. This became particularly clear when we tried out parts of our installation with preschoolers, who spent a long time engaging with the pearl curtain we used for the installation's centrepiece, simply because it was so nice to touch.

Still images from the light dance happening when all sensors on the installation are triggered at the same time.

Lessons learned

This was an incredibly fun project to work on for many reasons. Perhaps quite obviously because we had to bring back our inner children and try to remember what the thrill of the playground was. But also because it was a highly explorative project. We got to bring out a lot of practical skills we had long forgotten, such as soldering cables, connecting circuits and working with bench power supplies. A key takeaway being that everything can be solved and that one can master new skills at an impressive rate.

Coming from a background where I’ve produced a lot of wireframes and prototypes for apps and websites, it was really cool to get to work with physical models and all the opportunities and constraints that came with it. It definitely brought with it a newfound interest in physical product design and its user experience.

Outcomes

Our installation engaged people of all ages at the exhibition held at KTH. Visitors particularly enjoyed the multicoloured “full forest dance” and spent long periods of time interacting with the installation to trigger it. Because of some minor technology bugs that led to delayed reactions, the visitors had occasionally somewhat of a hard time figuring out how to trigger all sensors. However, we found that this led to a more playful environment among the visitors and to a higher degree made them cooperate with each other.
